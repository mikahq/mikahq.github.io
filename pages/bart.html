A Case of Convenience: Bay Area Rapid Transit

Understanding who uses the Bay Area Rapid Transit system and why.

Jump to: Research Goals; Methodology; Key Findings; Recommendations

Research Goals

The Bay Area Rapid Transit (BART) is a historic “heavy-rail public transit system” starting service in 1972. After the COVID-19 pandemic BART’s ridership fell drastically, covering only 25% of the costs related to maintenance and upkeep in 2023. It acts as an essential public transit service for a wide variety of Bay Area citizens and creates social and bureaucratic discourse in its function. By continuing new expansion into the San Jose area, BART is following its initial goal in “completing the [transist] loop” and answering the general desire for expanded coverage of the Bay Area, specifically in the South Bay. However, a host of issues exist with the currently running lines impacted by every-day maintenance.
  • With a change in the work/life balance, there exists a need to understand BART’s current and potential ridership and offer actionable improvements to the BART system.
  • This work is a call to engineers, architects, and policy makers to decide where best to spend money within our transit systems considering the voices of BART riders.

Methodology

I used a mix of the following qualitative research methods to gather data:

  • Interviews with target users, IDIs (N=5)
  • Desk research
  • Ethnography

Key Findings

    Confusion about BART’s identity
  • Riders often struggle to categorize BART, seeing it as a hybrid of subway, metro, and commuter rail, which affects their expectations of the system.
    • Public transit as a social experience
  • Many riders view BART as an inherently social experience, with interactions playing a significant role in their overall experience.
    • Complex relationship with safety and cleanliness
  • Riders’ perception of safety depends on the frequency of use—frequent riders feel safer, while infrequent riders associate BART with higher crime rates.
    • Perception of safety
  • Frequent riders feel a greater sense of safety, while less frequent riders associate BART with crime, largely due to negative word-of-mouth. Commuters appreciate the opportunity to travel with others, but crowded trains can feel welcoming, while emptiness may feel unsettling.
  • "...though I have the impression that oh, it might not be very reliable in that sense, and also that time I took the BART at night...There was a transition there and I remember we had to wait for 12 minutes maybe. Yeah, but it didn't feel that long in the at the time because I was with somebody. I could imagine if it's like at night and you're the only person there, it could be a little unsettling." -- BART rider

Despite vocal concerns about operational and environmental factors, BART still cultivates a somewhat transactional loyalty with its riders. The perspectives surrounding the reputation of BART were the most conflicting. One participant said reflectively:

“And I think that will always be the story of BART…people in general are thankful to have it. But nonetheless, it's going to be underfunded and neglected and really hard to improve.”

Ultimately BART inspires a complex relationship– one that also invites frustration. Perhaps, as the adage goes, to be known is to be loved. Participants that rode BART daily had more specific recommendations to offer, like talking about noise levels or artistic improvements. They were used to the environment BART offered. To them, BART is a grand idea, but its execution is difficult. This is reflected in one participant’s outlook on the South Bay expansion: “I think, though, in general, it's a worthy thing, it's just kind of disappointing how long [the expansion] will take.” These concepts of loyalty, and worth, suggest there could be a pattern of dedication towards BART and even other public services, like public radio, libraries, or other like-minded and/or useful institutions.

Recommendations

    Revive the art of maintenance
  • Dedicate actionable plans to the maintenance and upkeep of current BART systems, potentially prioritizing this over expansion efforts in the South Bay.
    • Create community involvement
  • Leverage the social aspect of public transportation by engaging the community in the upkeep and enhancement of BART systems.
    • Implement cleaning protocols
  • Update and enforce clean-station protocols to ensure a welcoming environment for all riders, such as adding more trash cans both inside stations and nearby.
    • Public art installations
  • Install local artwork in stations (e.g., Oakland, Downtown Berkeley) to build community engagement, similar to New York City’s subway art installations.
