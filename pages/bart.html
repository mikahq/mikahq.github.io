Solution

Confusion about BART‚Äôs classifcation as a transit system lead to dissatisfaction around wait times.

  • Riders often struggle to categorize BART, seeing it as a hybrid of subway, metro, and commuter rail, which affects their expectations of the system.

    • Solution: Create a marketing strategy to change riders' expectations and increase satisfaction.

Public transit is an inherently social experience.
  • Interactions with others play a significant role in rider's overall experience, with BART used most often on weekdays, such as Tuesdays, and used least on weekends. Commuters appreciate the opportunity to travel with others, but crowded trains can feel welcoming, while emptiness may feel unsettling

    • Solution: Improve aesthetics. Install local artwork in stations (e.g., Oakland, Downtown Berkeley) to build community engagement, similar to New York City‚Äôs subway art installations.

Transactional Loyalty

Despite vocal concerns about operational and environmental factors, BART still cultivates a somewhat transactional loyalty with its riders. The perspectives surrounding the reputation of BART were the most conflicting. One participant said reflectively:

‚ÄúAnd I think that will always be the story of BART‚Ä¶people in general are thankful to have it. But nonetheless, it's going to be underfunded and neglected and really hard to improve.‚Äù

Participant feelings of loyalty and worth, suggest there could be a pattern of dedication towards BART and even other public services, like public radio, libraries, or other like-minded and/or community focused institutions.

Methodology

Participants represented people who lived in the Bay Area in the last year or more, and used the BART at least once a month. I used a mix of the following qualitative research methods to gather data.

  • Desk research
  • Interviews with target users, IDIs (N=5)
  • Ethnography

Scope

What this project is, and what it isn't.

It is:

a brief qualitative overview of how Bay Area riders feel about the current state of BART in 2024.

What it is not:

Questions could have been more narrowly tailored about actionable items to improve BART from the participants.

More IDIs representing different BART riders based on frequency of use, or attitudinal/behavioral attributes would have made the study more robust.

What is left:

Many things. Among them are, how do the needs of BART riders geninuely impact their habits? Who used to ride BART, but doesn't now, and why? Who started to use BART after COVID? Why? Who does BART aim to serve, and who are they serving now?
