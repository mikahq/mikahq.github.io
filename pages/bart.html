A Case of Convenience: Bay Area Rapid Transit

Understanding why people typically ride the Bay Area Rapid Transit system.

  • Research Goals
  • Research Methodology
  • Key Findings
  • Recommendations

Research Goals

  • The Bay Area Rapid Transit (BART) is a historic “heavy-rail public transit system” starting service in 1972. After the COVID-19 pandemic BART’s ridership fell drastically, covering only 25% of the costs related to maintenance and upkeep in 2023. 
  • With a change in the work/life balance, there exists a need to understand BART’s current and potential ridership and offer actionable improvements to the BART system.

Research Methodology

I used a mix of the following qualitative research methods to gather data:

  • Interviews with target users, IDIs
  • Desk research
  • Ethnography

Key Findings

  • Confusion about BART’s identity: Riders often struggle to categorize BART, seeing it as a hybrid of subway, metro, and commuter rail, which affects their expectations of the system.
  • Public transit as a social experience: Many riders view BART as an inherently social experience, with interactions playing a significant role in their overall experience.
  • Complex relationship with safety and cleanliness: Riders’ perception of safety depends on the frequency of use—frequent riders feel safer, while infrequent riders associate BART with higher crime rates.
  • Crowded vs. empty trains: Commuters appreciate the opportunity to travel with others, but crowded trains can feel welcoming, while emptiness may feel unsettling.
  • Perception of safety: Frequent riders feel a greater sense of safety, while less frequent riders associate BART with crime, largely due to negative word-of-mouth.

Recommendations

  • The art of maintenance: Dedicate actionable plans to the maintenance and upkeep of current BART systems, potentially prioritizing this over expansion efforts in the South Bay.
  • Create community involvement: Leverage the social aspect of public transportation by engaging the community in the upkeep and enhancement of BART systems.
  • Implement cleaning protocols: Update and enforce clean-station protocols to ensure a welcoming environment for all riders, such as adding more trash cans both inside stations and nearby.
  • Public art installations: Install local artwork in stations (e.g., Oakland, Downtown Berkeley) to build community engagement, similar to New York City’s subway art installations.
